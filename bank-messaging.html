Bank Messaging

How can scam alert communications prevent harm by building trust?

Lead Researcher · 10 weeks · Qualitative + quantitative research

Needs and Goal

The experience design, marketing, and fraud departments partnered to revamp our messaging strategy. With over 30 different types of communications around fraud, we needed to standardize our approach in order to increase customer engagement and message credibility.

Business Goal Reduce false verification rates (where customers answer that a transaction is valid before filing a claim), and reduce customers scammed.

Why It Mattered

Fraud and scams cost customers and the bank billions of dollars. Our digital communications are the first line between customers' money and bad actors; they need to be simple to read and respond to. We set out to define a strategy for successful scam messaging.

Constraints & Tradeoffs

  • Limited resources for scope of project
  • Sparse historical data on urgency and mindsets
  • Could not run live tests at this stage

Method Selection

I conducted competitive research and stakeholder interviews in order to define the research scope. During this stage, I realized we needed a better understanding of emotional reactions and mental models, instead of the planned quantitative measurement of copy performance. It was time for a pivot.
First, I defined four message dimensions for investigation: Clarity, Trust, Urgency, and Action. I drafted a "menu", listing potential studies based on their outcomes and time requirements.
Finally, I held a workshop to align partner expectations around results and timing, where we settled on the plan I recommended:

  • Click test with simulated urgency to inform a design decision and provide additional direction for the follow-up study
  • Lean think-aloud study that would provide valuable behavioral insights, without the time commitment of scheduling interviews.

What We Found

How to connect to dimensions here... As expected, the dimensions overlapped when it came to mental models.

  • Customers primarily look for consistency cues as well as personal details when they decide to trust a message outside of their account. Yet scam education subconsciously contributes to trust. In the words of a participant, "A scammer wouldn't tell you that".

Customers are less likely to trust or act on a message when:

  • they don't recognize a listed transaction or detail
  • there's an unverified path - whether that's a link or phone number
  • they're not sure what's going to happen

Outcome & Impact

The team shifted from persistent alerts to contextual education. Messaging guidelines were adopted across future security features.

Reflection

This study reinforced how easily well-intentioned safeguards can undermine trust when emotional context is ignored.
